Beck’s coequaliser🔗

Let (F⊣U):C⇆D(F \dashv U) : \ca{C} \leftrightarrows \ca{D}(F⊣U):C⇆D be a pair of adjoint functors. Recall that every such adjunction induces a monad TTT (which we will abbreviate by TTT) on the category C\ca{C}C, and a “comparison” functor K:C→CTK : \ca{C} \to \ca{C}^{T}K:C→CT into the Eilenberg-Moore category of TTT. In this module we will lay out a sufficient condition for the functor $K to have a left adjoint, which we denote K−1K^{-1}K−1. Let us first establish a result about the presentation of TTT-algebras by “generators and relations.”

Suppose that we are given a TTT-algebra (A,ν)(A, \nu)(A,ν). Note that (TA,μ)(TA, \mu)(TA,μ) is also a TTT-algebra, namely the free TTT-algebra on the object AAA. Let us illustrate this with a concrete example: Suppose we have the cyclic group Z/nZ\bb{Z}/n\bb{Z}Z/nZ, for some natural number nnn, which we regard as a subgroup of Z\bb{Z}Z. The corresponding algebra (TA,μ)(TA, \mu)(TA,μ) would be the free group on one generator — the group of integers —, whence we conclude that, in general, this (TA,μ)(TA, \mu)(TA,μ) algebra is very far from being the (A,ν)(A, \nu)(A,ν) algebra we started with.

Still thinking about our Z/nZ\bb{Z}/n\bb{Z}Z/nZ example, it feels like we should be able to quotient the algebra (TA,μ)(TA, \mu)(TA,μ) by some set of relations and get back the algebra (A,ν)(A, \nu)(A,ν) we started with. This is absolutely true, and it’s true even when the category of TTT-algebras is lacking in quotients! In particular, we have the following theorem:

Theorem. Every TTT-algebra (A,ν)(A, \nu)(A,ν) is the coequaliser of the diagram

Furthermore, the arrows μ\muμ and TνT\nuTν have a common left inverse, so this is a reflexive coequaliser, called the Beck coequaliser.

The calculation is just.. well, calculation, so we present it without much further commentary. Observe that ν\nuν coequalises μ\muμ and TνT\nuTν essentially by the definition of being an algebra map.

      mult fold (record { morphism = A.ν ; commutes = sym A.ν-mult })

    A.ν C.∘ T.mult .η _ ≡˘⟨ A.ν-mult ⟩≡˘
    A.ν C.∘ T.M₁ A.ν    ∎

The colimiting map from (A,ν)(A, \nu)(A,ν) to some other algebra (F,ν′)(F, \nu')(F,ν′) which admits a map e′:(TA,μ)→(F,ν′)e' : (TA, \mu) \to (F, \nu')e′:(TA,μ)→(F,ν′) is given by the composite

A→ηTA→e′F, A \xto{\eta} TA \xto{e'} F\text{,} Aη​TAe′​F,

which is a map of algebras by a long computation, and satisfies the properties of a coequalising map by slightly shorter computations, which can be seen below. Uniqueness of this map follows almost immediately from the algebra laws.

Presented algebras🔗

The lemma above says that every algebra which exists can be presented by generators and relations; The relations are encoded by the pair of maps T2A⇉TAT^2A \tto TAT2A⇉TA in Beck’s coequaliser, above. But what about the converse? The following lemma says that, if every algebra presented by generators-and-relations (encoded by a parallel pair T2A⇉TAT^2A \tto TAT2A⇉TA) has a coequaliser in D\ca{D}D, then the comparison functor D→CT\ca{D} \to \ca{C}^TD→CT has a left adjoint.

On objects, this left adjoint acts by sending each algebra MMM to the coequaliser of (the diagram underlying) its Beck coequaliser. In a sense, this is “the best approximation in D\ca{D}D of the algebra.” The action on morphisms is uniquely determined since it’s a map out of a coequaliser.

If we consider the comparison functor as being “the TTT-algebra underlying an object of D\ca{D}D,” then the functor we construct below is the “free object of D\ca{D}D on a TTT-algebra.” Why is this adjunction relevant, though? Its failure to be an equivalence measures just how far our original adjunction is from being monadic, that is, how far D\ca{D}D is from being the category of TTT-algebras.

  Comparison⁻¹ : Functor (Eilenberg-Moore C T) D
  Comparison⁻¹ .F₀ = coapex ⊙ has-coeq
  Comparison⁻¹ .F₁ {X} {Y} alg-map =
    has-coeq X .coequalise {e′ = e′} path where
      e′ : D.Hom (F.F₀ (X .fst)) (Comparison⁻¹ .F₀ Y)
      e′ = has-coeq Y .coeq D.∘ F.₁ (alg-map .morphism)

The adjunction unit and counit are again very simple, and it’s evident to a human looking at them that they satisfy the triangle identities (and are algebra homomorphisms). Agda is not as easy to convince, though, so we leave the computation folded up for the bravest of readers.
